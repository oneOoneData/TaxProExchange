It's March. Your phone rings while you're three layers deep in a messy return, so you let it go to voicemail. That caller was a referral with a profitable S-corp — and by the time you call back tomorrow, they've booked with the preparer who picked up. Every missed call in season is a lead with a very short shelf life.

A **voice AI receptionist** answers every call instantly, day or night. It greets callers in your firm's name, fields the routine questions, qualifies the lead, books a consult straight into your calendar, and texts or emails you a summary. No PTO, no sick days, no busy-season burnout.

## Where it earns its keep

- **Capture during crunch** — you stop trading billable focus for a ringing phone.
- **After-hours and overflow** — a call at 8 p.m. or during a deep-work block still gets handled.
- **Consistent intake** — every caller gets the same qualifying questions, so your consult slots fill with real prospects instead of tire-kickers.

## What to look for

A natural-sounding voice, calendar integration, the ability to follow *your* script and FAQs, call summaries or transcripts, and a clean handoff to a human when the caller needs one. Test it by calling it yourself a dozen times with awkward questions before you ever trust it with a client.

The guardrails matter here too: a receptionist taking intake details is touching client information, so it has to meet your WISP and handle that data properly — and disclose that callers are speaking with an automated assistant where required. Keep it in its lane: greet, answer logistics, qualify, book. It does not give tax advice, and you still own every professional conclusion.

## Leading options

Most voice AI receptionist tools charge $100–$300/month for a solo practitioner setup. The key differentiators are: how natural the voice sounds on an actual call (test it), how well it handles interruptions and unexpected answers, and whether it integrates with your existing calendar and CRM.

Trial at least two before committing. A bad AI receptionist is worse than no receptionist — it frustrates callers and loses the leads it was supposed to capture.
